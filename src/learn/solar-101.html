Solar 101: How Solar Panels Work

Solar panels sit quietly on rooftops across the world, converting sunlight into usable electricity with no moving parts, no noise, and no emissions. Here is exactly how that process works — from photon to plug.

The Photovoltaic Effect: Light to Electricity

The photovoltaic (PV) effect was first observed by French physicist Edmond Becquerel in 1839, but it took until 1954 for Bell Labs to produce the first practical silicon solar cell with 6% efficiency. Today's commercial panels routinely exceed 21% efficiency, with lab records surpassing 47% for multi-junction cells (NREL Best Research-Cell Efficiency Chart, 2026).

Here is the physics in simple terms: A solar cell consists of two layers of silicon — one doped with phosphorus (n-type, extra electrons) and one doped with boron (p-type, electron "holes"). When sunlight (photons) strikes the cell, energy is transferred to electrons in the silicon lattice, knocking them loose. The electric field at the p-n junction forces these freed electrons to flow in one direction, creating direct current (DC). Metal contacts on the top and bottom of the cell collect this current and deliver it through external wiring.

A typical residential solar panel contains 60 or 72 individual solar cells wired in series, producing roughly 30-45 volts DC and 8-11 amps under full sun. The panel's rated wattage is simply voltage multiplied by current: a panel producing 40V at 10A outputs 400W.

The Complete Solar Energy Flow

  1. Solar Panels (Modules): Convert sunlight to DC electricity via the photovoltaic effect.
  2. Inverter: Converts DC to AC electricity compatible with your home and the utility grid. Modern inverters also perform MPPT (maximum power point tracking) to continuously optimize panel output throughout the day.
  3. Main Service Panel (Breaker Box): Distributes AC power to your home's circuits. Any excess flows back to the grid through your utility meter.
  4. Net Meter: A bidirectional meter that tracks both energy consumption from the grid and energy exported to the grid, enabling you to receive credit for surplus generation.
  5. Utility Grid: Acts as an infinite battery — absorbing your excess production during the day and supplying power at night. In most U.S. states, net metering policies determine how much credit you receive.

Key System Components

Solar Cells & Panels

The vast majority of residential panels use monocrystalline silicon cells, which offer the highest efficiency rates (20-23%) and the sleekest black appearance. Polycrystalline cells (15-18% efficiency) are less common in residential applications but remain popular in utility-scale projects where space is not a constraint. According to NREL's 2025 Annual Technology Baseline, the median residential panel efficiency reached 21.3% in 2025, up from 19.2% in 2020.

Inverter

The inverter is the brain of your solar system. Three architectures dominate the market: string inverters (one central unit per string of panels), microinverters (one small inverter per panel), and power optimizers (panel-level optimization with a central inverter). The choice depends on your roof's shading profile, orientation complexity, and budget. See our full Inverter Basics guide for a detailed comparison.

Mounting & Racking

Racking systems secure panels to your roof with aluminum rails and stainless steel fasteners. A properly installed racking system must withstand wind speeds of 110-140 mph (depending on local building codes) and typically carries a 10-25 year warranty. Ground-mount systems are an alternative when roof space is inadequate or structural issues exist.

Monitoring System

Modern solar installations include a monitoring system that tracks real-time and historical production data. Most systems offer smartphone apps showing daily generation, lifetime output, and environmental impact (CO₂ avoided). Enphase, SolarEdge, and Tesla all provide comprehensive monitoring platforms included with their hardware.

Grid-Tied vs. Off-Grid

Grid-tied systems (about 95% of U.S. residential installations) remain connected to the utility grid. They are the most cost-effective option because you can use the grid as backup and participate in net metering. The primary limitation: during a grid outage, grid-tied systems automatically shut down for utility worker safety (anti-islanding), unless paired with battery storage.

Off-grid systems operate independently of the grid and require battery storage plus a backup generator for reliability. They are suited for remote locations where grid connection is infeasible. The installed cost is typically 2-3x that of a comparable grid-tied system due to battery requirements.

Hybrid systems combine grid-tied operation with battery storage, providing backup power during outages while still enjoying net metering benefits. The Inflation Reduction Act's 30% ITC applies to battery storage even when retrofitted to existing solar, making hybrids increasingly attractive.

2026 Efficiency Landscape

The NREL chart of best research-cell efficiencies shows the remarkable progress of PV technology. As of early 2026, the record for single-junction crystalline silicon cells stands at 27.3% (LONGi Green Energy, 2024). Commercial monocrystalline panels from top manufacturers now deliver 20-23% module efficiency as standard:

  • Maxeon (SunPower) Maxeon 7: 24.1% module efficiency — the highest commercially available residential panel.
  • REC Alpha Pure-RX: 22.6% module efficiency with lead-free heterojunction technology (HJT).
  • Qcells Q.TRON BLK M-G2+: 22.0% module efficiency, manufactured in Georgia, USA.
  • Trina Vertex S+: 21.8% module efficiency with n-type TOPCon cell technology.

According to the IEA Photovoltaic Power Systems Programme (PVPS) Task 1 report, global average PV system prices fell to $0.88/W for utility-scale and approximately $2.70/W for residential rooftop installations in 2025, continuing the decade-long downward trend.

Installation Readiness Checklist

  • Roof condition: Is your roof less than 10 years old? If not, consider replacing it before installing solar to avoid removal/reinstallation costs ($2,000-$5,000).
  • Orientation and tilt: In the Northern Hemisphere, south-facing roofs at a 15-40° tilt are optimal. East/west-facing roofs can still be viable with 10-20% lower production.
  • Shade analysis: Check for shade from trees, chimneys, neighboring buildings between 9 AM and 3 PM. Even partial shading on a single panel can disproportionately reduce string inverter system output.
  • Energy consumption: Gather 12 months of electricity bills to calculate your average monthly kWh usage. This is the single most important data point for sizing your system.
  • Utility interconnection: Contact your utility to understand their net metering policy, interconnection fees, and any system size caps.
  • HOA and permits: Check HOA covenants and local permitting requirements. Many states have solar access laws that limit HOA restrictions, but the process varies.
  • Incentives research: The federal ITC is 30% through 2032 (phasing down to 26% in 2033 and 22% in 2034). Research state and local incentives through the DSIRE database.
