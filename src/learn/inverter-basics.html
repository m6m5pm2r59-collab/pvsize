Inverter Basics: String vs Microinverters vs Power Optimizers

Solar panels produce DC electricity. Your home and the grid run on AC. The inverter is the bridge between them — and choosing the right type is one of the most consequential decisions in any solar installation.

How Solar Inverters Work: DC to AC Conversion

Solar photovoltaic (PV) cells generate direct current (DC) when sunlight strikes the semiconductor material. Every appliance in your home, from the refrigerator to your laptop charger, operates on alternating current (AC). The inverter's core function is to convert DC to AC, but modern inverters do far more: they perform maximum power point tracking (MPPT) to optimize panel output, provide anti-islanding protection for grid safety, and stream performance data to your smartphone.

The conversion efficiency of an inverter — typically 95% to 99% for leading models — directly impacts your system's total energy yield. A 2% efficiency gap on a 10 kW system producing 14,000 kWh annually translates to roughly 280 kWh of lost generation each year, or about $42 at the 2026 U.S. average retail electricity rate of $0.15/kWh (EIA, May 2026).

Three Inverter Architectures Compared

1. String Inverters

A string inverter connects a series of panels (a "string") to a single central inverter, typically wall-mounted near the main service panel. This is the most mature and cost-effective architecture, accounting for roughly 55% of U.S. residential installations in 2025 (SEIA Solar Market Insight, Q4 2025). String inverters excel on roofs with uniform orientation, minimal shading, and no complex roof planes.

Key limitation: In a traditional string setup, the entire string performs at the level of its weakest panel. If one panel is shaded by a chimney or covered in leaves, the whole string's output drops. Modern string inverters with multiple MPPT channels (commonly 2-4) partially mitigate this by allowing different roof orientations on separate strings.

2. Microinverters

Microinverters are small inverters mounted directly beneath each individual solar panel, performing DC-to-AC conversion at the module level. This architecture eliminates the single-point-of-failure problem and allows each panel to operate independently at its maximum power point. Enphase Energy dominates this segment with its IQ8 series, which shipped over 7 million units globally in 2025 (Enphase 2025 Annual Report).

Microinverters are ideal for complex roofs with multiple orientations, partial shading, or installations where future expansion is anticipated. The trade-off is higher upfront cost — typically $0.25-$0.40/W more than string inverters. However, independent panel-level monitoring and a 25-year warranty (matching panel lifespans) are compelling advantages.

3. Power Optimizers (DC Optimizers)

Power optimizers occupy a middle ground: they are module-level power electronics (MLPE) that perform MPPT and voltage optimization at each panel, but the actual DC-to-AC conversion is handled by a central string inverter. SolarEdge is the market leader here, with its HD-Wave technology achieving a California Energy Commission (CEC) weighted efficiency of 99.0% for the SE7600H model.

The optimizer approach gives you panel-level MPPT like microinverters, but with fewer components that contain electrolytic capacitors (the most common failure point in power electronics). SolarEdge shipped 16.3 GW of inverters globally in 2025 (SolarEdge Q4 2025 Earnings).

How to Choose: Decision Framework

Choose a string inverter if: your roof faces one direction with no significant shade, your budget is tight, and you prefer proven, low-complexity technology. The Tesla Solar Inverter and SMA Sunny Boy series are excellent options here.

Choose microinverters if: your roof has multiple orientations, partial shade from trees or chimneys, you want panel-level monitoring, and you may expand the system later. Enphase IQ8 is the dominant choice in this category.

Choose power optimizers if: you want panel-level MPPT with a centralized inverter, prefer the SolarEdge ecosystem, or want the highest CEC-rated efficiency. This is the fastest-growing segment for complex residential roofs.

According to NREL's 2025 Annual Technology Baseline, the weighted-average installed cost for residential inverters (all types) has declined to approximately $0.28/W in 2025, down from $0.35/W in 2020 — a 20% reduction over five years driven by manufacturing scale and power electronics advances.
